Date Range

From / To

The calendar window of price data the backtest will trade. Only bars within this range generate trades — bars outside it are still used to warm up indicators.

IS / OOS Split

In-Sample (IS) is the period you optimise settings on. Out-of-Sample (OOS) is the period you validate on — it should never be touched during optimisation.

  • IS 70% — sets the end date to 70% through the full range. Use this to optimise settings.
  • OOS 30% — sets the start date to the IS cut-off. Run this after optimising to validate.
  • Reset — restores the original full date range.

Good practice: optimise on IS, lock settings, then run OOS once. If OOS metrics are similar to IS the strategy is robust. A large gap means over-fitting.

Strategy

Confluence Method

The model projects Fibonacci levels from the Asia session range (00:00–06:00 London) and from the prior day's range. A confluence is where today's fib and yesterday's fib land within the tolerance of each other — a structurally significant price cluster.

  • Asia only — uses today's Asia range fibs vs yesterday's.
  • Monday only — uses the current week's Monday range vs last week's Monday range. Longer-term structural levels.
  • Asia + Monday — merges both sets. More potential trades but also more noise.

Signal Filter

  • All confluences — trade any level where today's and yesterday's fibs overlap.
  • Tight only — only trade confluences where the two fibs are very close together (≤ tight-tol % of the main tolerance). Higher conviction, fewer trades.
  • All levels — ignore confluences entirely, trade at every enabled fib level. Maximum trades, least selectivity.

Confluence Tolerance (pips)

Maximum pip distance between today's fib and yesterday's fib for them to be counted as a confluence. Wider = more confluences found; tighter = only very precise overlaps.

Tight Tol %

A confluence is labelled "tight" if its actual fib distance is less than this percentage of the main tolerance. Used by the Tight only filter.

Min ATR dist from open

Entry only triggers when the confluence level is at least this many ATR multiples away from the daily open price. Prevents entering levels that are already at the open — those tend to be noise rather than clean S/D zones.

Warmup Days

Number of trading days at the start of the dataset that are used to prime the indicator windows (ATR, EMA, RSI etc.) but do not generate any trades. Set too low and early trades use poorly initialised indicators. 100–150 days is typical for M5 data.

Min Conviction

A score from -1 to +1 representing how strongly the enabled features agree on direction. Calculated as weighted votes: each feature that agrees with the chosen direction adds its weight; each that disagrees subtracts it. Score is then normalised by the total possible weight.

  • 0 — take any trade where features are net positive (default).
  • 0.3 — require 30% net agreement. Cuts marginal trades.
  • 0.5+ — strong conviction required. Fewer but higher-quality entries.

Min Confirms

Minimum number of individual features (regardless of weight) that must vote in the same direction. Prevents a single high-weight feature dominating. E.g. 3 means at least 3 separate features must agree.

Entry Window (London)

The time window in which new entries are allowed. All times are London local. Entries after 20:00 are always blocked to avoid holding through the US close. 08:00–20:00 captures the full London + New York overlap. 09:30 avoids the London open sweep but misses early moves.

Level Re-entry

How many times the engine can enter at the same confluence level on the same day.

  • Once only — one trade per level per day. Most conservative.
  • Twice — allows one re-entry after price leaves the level by ≥ 0.5 ATR and returns. Captures the "second touch" pattern.
  • Unlimited — re-enter any time price returns. Risk of repeated SL hits at the same level.

ATR Period

Number of bars used to compute the Average True Range. ATR is used for entry proximity, SL sizing (ATR mode), and the re-entry cleared-distance check. 14 is standard; shorter = more reactive, longer = smoother.

Entry Quality Filters

2nd-Touch Only

When enabled, the first bar that tags a confluence level is skipped — it is logged internally but no trade is placed. A trade is only entered on the second approach to the same level, after price has clearly left (moved ≥ 0.5 ATR away) and returned.

Why it helps: first touches often represent the initial liquidity sweep where weak longs/shorts are stopped out. The second touch tends to be a cleaner, higher-probability reversal because liquidity has already been taken.

Trade-off: significantly fewer trades — many days only produce one touch per level.

Require Liquidity Sweep

Before any entry at a level is allowed, the engine must have seen price wick through the level by at least the Sweep Depth amount and then close back on the other side on the same bar.

Why it helps: confirms that the stop-hunt / liquidity grab has already happened. You are entering the reversal after the sweep, not into it.

Sweep Depth (pips)

How far price must pierce through the level (in pips) for the bar to count as a sweep. 2 pips is a minimal pierce; 5+ requires a more aggressive stop-hunt. Too large and very few sweeps will qualify.

Candle Confirm (N bars)

After price touches the level, the engine waits for the next N M1 bars to close before deciding whether to enter. 0 disables this check entirely (immediate entry).

Example: set N=3 and threshold=0.6 → after the touch bar, 3 M1 bars must close: if at least 2 of 3 close bullish (for a long) entry triggers on bar 4. This is your M1 candle confirmation gate.

Trade-off: entry is later, so price may have already moved. Works best at high-conviction levels where the reversal is decisive.

Confirm Threshold (%)

The fraction of the N confirmation bars that must close in the trade direction. 0.6 = 60% (3 of 5). 1.0 = every bar must confirm — very strict. 0.5 = simple majority.

M1 Precision Features

By default, intraday features (Wick Rejection, RSI Divergence, Order Block, FVG Bias, TWAP Slope, MACD) compute off the M5 bar window. Enabling this toggle switches those 7 features to use M1 bars instead, giving higher precision at each entry without needing a separate M1 data file — as long as M1 data is loaded.

Why it reduces bias: M5 bars used at 09:05 contain data from 09:00–09:05, which isn't look-ahead. But the subtle issue is that the M5 window contains earlier bars whose structure was formed in hindsight relative to intraday decisions. M1 bars give the engine the exact same view of price that a live trader would have at that moment — each bar is the most recent closed M1 candle only.

Trade-off: Structural features (CHoCH/BOS, ADX, Hurst, Ichimoku, HTF EMA, Weekly Pivot) deliberately stay on M5/M30/daily — M1 swings are too noisy to define meaningful structure. M1 mode gives more entry-precision but can also amplify M1-level noise in the intraday signals. A/B test both and compare Bayesian accuracy per feature.

Stop Loss

SL Calculation Mode

  • 5m ATR — stop distance = ATR multiplier × current 5m ATR. Tight and reactive to current volatility. Good for fast-moving pairs (GBP/USD, XAU).
  • 30m ATR — same but uses the 30m ATR, which is wider and smoother. Gives trades more breathing room at the cost of a worse R-ratio.
  • Range-based — stop distance = a fraction of today's Asia range. Structurally anchored: if the range was 40 pips and fraction is 0.35, SL is 14 pips. More consistent across market conditions.

SL Multiplier (ATR modes)

How many ATR multiples wide the stop is. 1.0 = exactly 1 ATR. 1.5 gives extra room for typical intra-bar noise. Increase if you get stopped out on valid setups before the move continues.

SL Fraction (Range mode)

Fraction of the Asia range used as the stop distance. 0.35 = 35% of the range. The range is a natural volatility proxy for the day — this keeps stops proportional to the day's expected movement.

Min SL Floor (pips)

The absolute minimum stop distance regardless of ATR or range. Prevents absurdly tight stops on very quiet days when ATR or range is near zero. 5 pips on EUR/USD is typical; use 20–30 for Gold.

Note: all SL distances are calculated from the confluence level price, not from the current bar close. Entry is assumed at the exact level.

Take Profit

TP Calculation Mode

  • Fixed R-multiple — TP is placed at exactly R × SL distance from entry. Simple, consistent. Use 2.0R minimum to maintain a positive expectancy with a 40%+ win rate.
  • Structural — TP is placed just before the next confluence level in the trade direction (minus the buffer). Targets a real support/resistance cluster rather than an arbitrary distance. Better than fixed R when levels are clearly spaced.
  • Vol-Scaled R — TP target adjusts based on the day's volatility regime. Low-vol days get a tighter target (less follow-through); high-vol days get a larger target. Regime is determined by the Asia range pip size.

R-Multiple (Fixed R)

The reward-to-risk ratio. 2.2 means TP is 2.2× the SL distance. With a 35% win rate, 2.2R is approximately breakeven after costs. You need ~40% to be profitable at 2.2R.

TP Buffer (Structural mode)

Pips subtracted from the next confluence level to avoid placing TP exactly on a level where price might stall. 5 pips gives a cushion so the order fills before price rejects at the level.

Vol-Scaled R targets

The R-multiples for each volatility regime. Asia range determines regime: High vol >50 pips, Med vol 25–50 pips, Low vol <25 pips. Set high-vol target larger since trending conditions support more follow-through.

TP Fallback (× ATR)

Used in Structural mode when no confluence exists in the trade direction. Falls back to ATR × this multiplier as the TP distance. Prevents trades with no TP from being placed.

Re-enter at Confluence after TP

When price hits TP at a structural confluence level, that level is immediately flagged as "cleared" — so if price pulls back to it the same day, the engine can re-enter without waiting for the normal cleared-distance check.

⚡ Flip Trade on SL

When a trade hits SL, the engine immediately opens the opposite direction at the same level. The logic: if your fade (mean-reversion) was stopped out, price has broken through — treat it as a confirmed breakout and trade in the breakout direction.

When it works well: strong trend days where the initial reversal trade was wrong but the breakout follow-through is significant.

Risk: if price whipsaws around the level, you take two losses. The flip trade has no feature-vote filter — it is directional only. Disable in ranging/choppy market regimes.

SD Level Filter

SD (Supply/Demand) levels are the Fibonacci projections of the Asia range. Each fib number represents a structural price zone relative to the range.

Above range — Sell-side

Levels projected above the Asia range high. Price entering this zone is entering potential supply. These are short bias levels — fading a move above range back toward the range.

Within range — Mid

Levels inside the Asia range (0.0 to 1.0 fibs). These are mean-reversion levels within the established range. Lower signal quality on average since price is less committed to a direction.

Below range — Buy-side

Levels projected below the Asia range low. Price entering this zone is entering potential demand. These are long bias levels — fading a move below range back toward the range.

Preset chips

  • Sell-side — enable only above-range levels. Best in bearish macro environments.
  • Buy-side — enable only below-range levels. Best in bullish macro environments.
  • Mid — enable only within-range levels. Useful for range-bound days.

Tip: start with All enabled, run the Bayesian Feature Accuracy table, then check which specific SD levels have the highest win rates and enable only those.

Features

Features are technical signals that vote on trade direction. Each enabled feature returns long, short, or abstain. Votes are weighted and summed to produce a conviction score and direction. A feature that abstains does not penalise conviction — only active disagreement does.

Range Position

Is price currently above or below the Asia range midpoint? Above mid → short bias (expect return to range); below mid → long bias. A simple but powerful mean-reversion signal.

CHoCH / BOS

Change of Character / Break of Structure. Looks at recent M5 swing highs and lows. A CHoCH is a reversal signal (price fails to continue the prior swing direction). A BOS is a continuation/breakout signal. Weighted at 2 by default — it has high structural significance.

Wick Rejection

Examines the most recent M5 bar. A long lower wick (body near the top of the bar) signals buying pressure; a long upper wick signals selling pressure. Fires when the wick is ≥ 60% of the bar's total range.

RSI Divergence

Compares price action to RSI on M5. If price makes a lower low but RSI makes a higher low → bullish divergence (long signal). If price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high → bearish divergence (short signal). Divergence often precedes reversals at key levels.

Order Block

Identifies the last M5 bar that moved aggressively in one direction before a significant counter-move. That bar's body becomes an "order block" — institutional buy/sell zone. Vote is based on whether current price is testing a bullish or bearish order block.

HTF EMA 21/50

Uses 21-period and 50-period EMAs on M30 bars. If EMA21 > EMA50 → bullish trend context (long bias). If EMA21 < EMA50 → bearish (short bias). Aligns entries with the higher-timeframe trend direction.

TWAP Slope

Time-Weighted Average Price computed on the M5 bars since the open. Measures the slope over the last N bars. Rising TWAP → price is spending more time above average (long bias). Falling TWAP → short bias. Filters entries that are fighting the intraday trend.

ADX Filter

Average Directional Index on M30. If ADX < 20 → market is ranging, abstain (no directional signal). If ADX ≥ 20 → trending: uses DI+ vs DI− crossover to determine direction. Prevents trend-following in choppy conditions.

Hurst Regime

Estimates the Hurst exponent from daily closes. H < 0.45 → mean-reverting regime (good for this strategy, abstains to avoid conflicting). H > 0.55 → trending regime, votes in the direction of recent price momentum. H near 0.5 → random walk, abstains.

FVG Bias

Fair Value Gap. Identifies M5 bars where price gapped between the prior bar's high/low and the next bar's low/high. Price tends to return to fill these gaps. Vote direction is based on whether current price is below an unfilled bearish FVG (short) or above an unfilled bullish FVG (long).

Weekly Pivot

Classic pivot point calculated from last week's OHLC. Price above weekly pivot → long bias; below → short bias. Adds a swing-trading context to intraday entries.

Ichimoku Cloud

Uses the Ichimoku Cloud (Tenkan/Kijun/Senkou) on daily bars. Price above the cloud → bullish; below → bearish; inside the cloud → abstain. A broad trend-bias filter.

MACD (12/26/9)

Standard MACD on M5. Signal line crossover determines direction: MACD above signal → long; below → short. Captures momentum shifts but can lag at turning points.

Z-Score

Mirrors the VanHe1sing Pine Script indicator exactly: zscore = (close − SMA(close, N)) / StdDev(close, N). Measures how many standard deviations price is from its rolling mean.

  • Long Z ≤ threshold (e.g. −2.0): price is 2σ below the mean → statistically oversold → vote long
  • Short Z ≥ threshold (e.g. +2.0): price is 2σ above the mean → statistically overbought → vote short
  • Between the two thresholds → abstain (no directional edge)

Best use: combine with Range Position and Wick Rejection for a high-conviction mean-reversion entry. Tighten thresholds (±1.5) for more trades, widen (±2.5) for fewer but higher-probability signals. Runs on M1 when M1 precision mode is on.

💡 Check the Bayesian Feature Accuracy table after a run to see each feature's real win rate on your data. Disable features with win rates below 45% — they are adding noise rather than signal.

Hybrid Options
By default trades must go toward the Asia range midpoint (pure mean-reversion). Tick this to allow entries in either direction — letting the feature votes and level context decide. Enables trend-continuation pullback trades at fib levels.
Telegram Filters

These filters mirror the Telegram alert logic so you can backtest only the trades that would actually be sent as alerts. Enable a filter to restrict entries to those that meet the corresponding criterion.

Min Stars

Stars come from confluence quality: 1 base + 1 for Tight Fib + 1 for Round Number + 1 for cluster density ≥2. Setting 2★ keeps only Tight or clustered levels.

Min Grade

The grade mirrors gradeEntry() in trade-grade.js. Score is derived from feature conviction. A+ requires ≥72% conviction + no warnings. A requires ≥60%. B requires ≥46%. C is everything else.

Min Signal %

Filters by signal strength score (0–100). The backtest uses a conviction proxy: score = 50 + conviction × 50. This mirrors the Telegram's min_signal_score filter. 55%+ is a reasonable baseline; 65%+ is strong.

CHoCH/BOS Aligned

Requires the CHoCH/BOS feature to agree with the trade direction. This is equivalent to the "30m BOS" line in the Telegram message agreeing with entry direction.

Min ADX

Requires the ADX value from the ADX Filter feature to be above the threshold. Useful for filtering out low-momentum, choppy range environments.

WT1 Aligned

Requires the WaveTrend oscillator (WT1) on M5 to agree with trade direction — WT1 > 0 for longs, WT1 < 0 for shorts.

Range Bias Net+

Requires more features confirming the direction than conflicting with it (confirmCount > conflictCount). Filters out trades where direction is contested.

Position Sizing

Fixed (£/pip)

Every trade uses the same £-per-pip value regardless of stop size. Simple but does not compound — a wider stop loses more £ for the same R. Good for comparing strategies on a level playing field. The equity curve shows cumulative R only, so sizing doesn't affect the R-based statistics.

% Risk (compound)

Each trade risks a fixed percentage of the current account equity. Position size is adjusted so that if SL hits, you lose exactly Risk % of the account. Profits compound: winning periods grow position sizes, losing periods shrink them. This is the realistic live-trading approach.

Note: compounding amplifies both winning and losing streaks. A 10-trade losing run at 1% risk costs ~9.6% of account. At 2% risk it costs ~18.3%.

Transaction Costs

Transaction costs are deducted from each trade's R value after the raw result is calculated. They are expressed in pips and converted to R-fraction using the trade's SL distance.

Spread (pips)

The bid-ask spread paid on entry. Typical values: EUR/USD 0.8, GBP/USD 1.0, XAU/USD 25 (in price, not pips — check your broker). This is the most significant cost on short-hold intraday trades.

Slippage (pips)

Extra cost from imperfect fills — the difference between the signal price and actual execution price. At liquid London hours this is usually <0.5 pips for FX majors. Higher during news events. Simulates real execution quality.

Commission (£/lot)

Round-trip broker commission per standard lot (100,000 units). Set to 0 if your broker uses spread-only pricing. Typical ECN commission: £3–7 per lot per side (£6–14 round trip). For a 10-pip SL with £10/pip, one lot loses £100 → £7 commission is 7% of the risk.

Break-even Cost

After the run, the results panel shows the maximum total cost (spread + slippage) that the strategy can absorb before going flat. If your real costs exceed this number, the strategy is not viable at that cost level.

Kill Switches

Kill switches stop all new entries for the remainder of a period once cumulative losses reach the threshold. They simulate the risk management discipline of a real trader stopping out for the day/week/month.

Daily max loss (R)

If cumulative R on the current day falls to −N, no more entries are taken for the rest of that day. Example: set to 2 → after losing 2R in a day, trading stops. Prevents catastrophic drawdown days.

Weekly max loss (R)

Same logic but accumulates across the week (Mon–Fri). Resets every Monday. Use to simulate a "rule" like "never lose more than 5R in a week."

Monthly max loss (R)

Accumulates across the calendar month. Resets on the 1st. A monthly drawdown limit of 10R is common in prop firm rules.

Setting to 0 disables that kill switch. Start by testing without kill switches to see the raw strategy performance. Then add them to see their impact — they typically reduce both the worst drawdowns and total returns.

Optimal values can be found by testing: if the strategy has a max single-day loss of 3R historically, a daily kill at 2.5R prevents the worst outlier days without triggering too often.

0 = disabled · e.g. 2 = stop after −2R that period

Summary Statistics

Trades / Wins / Losses

Raw trade counts. A healthy backtest needs at least 200+ trades for statistical significance. Below 100 trades, any win rate figure is mostly noise.

Win Rate

Percentage of trades that closed at TP (positive R). At a 2.2R target you need ~32% to break even, ~40%+ to be profitable after costs. Win rate alone is meaningless — it must be interpreted alongside the R-multiple.

Profit Factor

Total gross profit ÷ total gross loss. >1.5 is good, >2.0 is excellent. Values below 1.0 mean the strategy loses money overall. Unlike win rate, PF captures both the frequency and magnitude of wins/losses.

Mean R

Average R per trade (net of costs). Positive mean R is the core requirement. Multiply by expected trades per year to estimate annual R return. E.g. 0.15R mean × 250 trades = ~37R/year.

Sharpe Ratio

Mean R ÷ standard deviation of R, annualised. Measures return per unit of risk. >1 is acceptable, >2 is strong, >3 is exceptional. High Sharpe means consistent returns without wild swings.

Calmar Ratio

Annualised return ÷ maximum drawdown. >1 means you earn back your max drawdown every year. Higher is better. Low Calmar with high Sharpe suggests a slow grind; high Calmar suggests efficient use of risk.

Max Drawdown

Largest peak-to-trough decline in the equity curve (as a % of peak equity at 1% risk per trade). This is the number that determines whether you can psychologically and financially survive the strategy's worst losing run. <15% is manageable; >30% is very hard to trade live.

Kelly %

The theoretically optimal fraction of account to risk per trade to maximise geometric growth. In practice use half Kelly or less. If Kelly is <1% or negative, the strategy's edge is marginal or negative.

CAGR

Compound Annual Growth Rate at 1% risk per trade on a £1 starting account. A normalised measure of annual return that accounts for compounding. Use this to compare strategies across different test lengths.

Monte Carlo — 1,000 path resamples (P5 / P25 / P50 / P75 / P95)

Monte Carlo simulation randomly reorders the actual trade outcomes 1,000 times to show the range of possible equity paths the strategy could produce. It answers: "if the trades happened in a different sequence, how different would the outcome be?"

Bands (P5 to P95)

  • P50 — median path. Half of all simulations did better, half worse.
  • P75/P95 — lucky sequences. Shows upside if you hit a good run of trades.
  • P25/P5 — unlucky sequences. P5 is the worst 5% of paths — this is what you plan for.

What to look for

  • If P5 final equity is still positive, the strategy survives its worst realistic sequence.
  • A wide band between P5 and P95 means outcome is highly sequence-dependent — consider reducing position size.
  • A narrow band means the edge is consistent and sequence risk is low.

Bayesian Feature Accuracy

For each feature, this table tracks every trade where that feature fired (voted non-null) and whether the trade was a winner. This is the empirical win rate conditional on that feature agreeing with the direction taken.

Fires

Number of trades where this feature cast a vote (long or short — not abstained). A feature with very few fires may not have enough data to be reliable.

Win Rate

% of fired trades that were winners. >55% suggests the feature genuinely improves signal quality. <45% suggests it is adding noise — consider disabling it. Close to 50% means it has no predictive edge.

How to use this

  • Disable features with win rates consistently below 45% across multiple runs.
  • Increase the weight of features with win rates above 60% — they deserve more influence on the conviction score.
  • If a feature has <50 fires, its win rate is too noisy to trust — enable more data or a longer date range first.
  • Re-run after changing weights and compare: if overall win rate and PF improve, the weight change was beneficial.

Note: win rate here counts the trade outcome (TP vs SL), not whether the feature itself was "right" about direction. A feature that fires correctly but entry timing was poor may still show low win rate.

SD Level Breakdown

Aggregates every trade across the full run by the Fibonacci SD level where entry occurred. Use this to find which levels have the highest win rate and best R expectancy — and which to filter out.

  • SD Level — Fibonacci projection from the Asia session range. SD0 = range low, SD1 = range high, SD±0.5 = midpoints inside, SD2+ = outside the range (extension zones).
  • Win Rate — % of trades at that level that hit TP. Higher is better. Levels below 40% consistently are candidates for disabling via the Fib filter.
  • Total R / Avg R — net R earned at this level across all trades. A high win rate with negative total R means winners are smaller than losers — check the R:R setting.
Trade Log (last 200 trades)

Shows the most recent 200 trades in chronological order. Click any column header to sort. Click ▶ on a row to expand its feature vote breakdown.

Columns

  • Date — London date the trade was entered.
  • Dir — trade direction: ↑ Long (buying) or ↓ Short (selling).
  • Entry / Exit — exact price at entry (confluence level) and at close (TP, SL, or EOD price).
  • SD Level — the Fibonacci SD level where entry occurred (e.g. SD1.5 = 150% fib above range). ↔ shows yesterday's fib if different from today's.
  • Result — TP hit take profit · SL hit stop loss · EOD closed at market at 21:00.
  • R — profit/loss in R-multiples. +2.2R means you made 2.2× your risk. −1R means you lost your full stop.

Feature vote pills (expanded row)

Each pill shows one feature's vote for that trade: green = agreed with direction taken, red = disagreed, faded = abstained. The number at the right of each pill (+1, −1, 0) is the weighted contribution to the conviction score.

Use this to understand why a trade was taken and which features were wrong on losing trades — this is the key to refining feature weights.
