How the Hedge Score is Calculated

A hedge pair is two FX instruments that move in opposite directions when macro risk conditions shift. This page scores every possible pair-pair combination — below is a complete walkthrough of every calculation, with worked numbers you can verify in a spreadsheet.

1
Log Returns — Why Not Simple % Changes?

Every calculation in this model starts with log returns (also called continuously compounded returns). For a price series P, the log return at time t is:

rt  =  ln( Pt  /  Pt−1 )

For a 4-hour bar close of 1.0851 after a previous close of 1.0823:
r = ln(1.0851 / 1.0823) = ln(1.002587) ≈ +0.002584

Why log returns and not % returns?
Two reasons. First, log returns are time-additive: a 10-day log return equals the sum of ten daily log returns, making rolling windows mathematically clean. Second, they are symmetric: a +5% move and a −5% reversal are equal in magnitude in log space (both ≈ 0.0488), whereas simple returns are asymmetric (+5% then −4.76% to return to start). Pearson correlation and OLS regression both assume symmetric error distributions, so log returns are the correct input.
Worked Example — EURUSD log returns over 5 H4 bars

These four return values [+0.002584, −0.001199, +0.003046, −0.001381] become the X series fed into Step 2. In production the model uses a 60-bar rolling window (~10 trading days on H4).

2
Pearson Correlation of Log Returns

Once we have log return series for two pairs — call them X (e.g. EURUSD) and Y (e.g. USDCHF) — over the rolling window, we compute the Pearson correlation coefficient r. This measures the linear co-movement between the two series and ranges from −1 to +1.

Pearson r
rXY =
Σᵢ (xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)
√[ Σᵢ(xᵢ − x̄)²  ·  Σᵢ(yᵢ − ȳ)² ]
xᵢ , yᵢLog return of pair X and pair Y at bar i
x̄ , ȳMean log return of each series over the full window
Σᵢ ( · )Sum over all N bars (model requires N ≥ 10)
r ∈ [−1, +1]Result is clamped — if numerical noise pushes it outside ±1 it is clipped
What each part does
The numerator is the covariance (un-normalised): positive when both series move in the same direction, negative when they move opposite. The denominator is the product of both series' standard deviations — it scales the covariance to [−1, +1] so the result is comparable across pairs with different volatilities.

r ≈ −1.0 confirms that EURUSD and USDCHF move almost perfectly in opposite directions over this window — a textbook natural hedge. In practice, stable pairs typically show r in the range −0.7 to −0.9.

3
VIX Beta — OLS Regression Against a Fear Proxy

The VIX beta (βvix) measures how sensitively a pair responds to changes in market fear. It is estimated by regressing each pair's log returns against a VIX proxy using Ordinary Least Squares (OLS).

VIX proxy
VIX proxy returnₜ = −1 × ln( USDCHFt / USDCHFt−1 )
Why USDCHF × −1?
The Swiss Franc is a classic safe-haven currency. When fear spikes, capital flows into CHF, causing USDCHF to fall. Multiplying by −1 inverts the series so the proxy rises when fear rises — mirroring the intuition of VIX (where a higher reading = more fear / risk-off).

The OLS formula gives the slope of the regression line — how many units the pair return moves for each unit move in the fear proxy:

OLS beta
β  =
Σᵢ (xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)
Σᵢ (xᵢ − x̄)²
xVIX proxy log returns (the independent variable / "input")
yPair log returns (the dependent variable / "output")
β > 0Risk-ON — pair rises when fear rises (e.g. GBPUSD tends to rally with equities)
β < 0Risk-OFF / Safe-haven — pair falls when fear rises (e.g. USDJPY falls because JPY is bid)
β ≈ 0Macro-neutral — pair is driven by idiosyncratic factors, not risk sentiment

The same 60-bar rolling window and 10-bar minimum used for Pearson also applies to OLS beta.

4
Beta Spread — Quantifying Opposite Risk Profiles

The beta spread captures how differently two pairs respond to fear. A large spread means one pair is strongly risk-on and the other is strongly risk-off — exactly what a macro hedge needs.

β Spread  =  | βvix(A)  −  βvix(B) |
Example — EURUSD (A) vs USDCHF (B)
EURUSD βvix = +0.82   (risk-on, rises with equity / risk sentiment)
USDCHF βvix = −0.71   (safe-haven, falls when fear rises)
β Spread = | 0.82 − (−0.71) | = | 1.53 | = 1.53

A spread of 1.53 is large — these pairs sit on opposite ends of the risk-sentiment spectrum. A spread near zero means both pairs have similar risk profiles and offer little hedging benefit beyond what the correlation already captures.

5
The Hedge Score — Combining Both Signals

The hedge score blends correlation (70% weight) and beta spread (30% weight) into a single ranking number. A high score means the two pairs are both statistically opposite and have opposite macro risk profiles.

Hedge Score
Score  =  (−r  ×  0.7)  +  ( β Spread  ÷  2  ×  0.3 )
−r × 0.7Correlation component. Sign is negated so r = −1 (perfect negative correlation) contributes +0.70 (maximum). r = +1 contributes −0.70 (worst possible).
β Spread ÷ 2 × 0.3Beta spread component. Divided by 2 to normalise — a spread of 2.0 contributes +0.30. A spread of 0 contributes nothing.
Full Worked Example — EURUSD vs USDCHF
Average Pearson r (over full history) = −0.82
EURUSD βvix = +0.82  |  USDCHF βvix = −0.71
β Spread = | 0.82 − (−0.71) | = 1.53
Correlation component: −(−0.82) × 0.7 = +0.574
Beta spread component: (1.53 / 2) × 0.3 = +0.230
Score = 0.574 + 0.230 = 0.804 → Strong Hedge

Practical trading note
A high hedge score means you trade the two pairs in opposite directions — long A and short B (or vice versa), sized so that the notional dollar exposure is approximately equal on both legs. This cancels out macro factor risk (DXY, rates, risk sentiment) while preserving alpha from the relative mispricing between the two pairs. The score does not tell you which direction to trade — it only tells you which pairs offset each other.
